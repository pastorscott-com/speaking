Random pastor\/funeral thoughts:<\/p>\n

    \n
  1. Spend an hour or so with the family early on \u2013 during that time, plan the service, but also let them tell you things \u2013 take notes \u2013 use some of what they said in the sermon.<\/li>\n
  2. Always open the service yourself.\u00a0 Introduce yourself even in your own church \u2013 this isn\u2019t your Sunday crowd.\u00a0 Welcome people and on behalf of the family and your congregation thank them for coming.<\/li>\n
  3. Be in charge.\u00a0 Do an order of service and give a copy to everyone.\u00a0 The Funeral Director will appreciate it too.<\/li>\n
  4. If you have guest clergy assisting, invite him or her to read the obituary and then offer personal remarks.<\/li>\n
  5. Take a text and preach a real sermon as described in the previous post.<\/li>\n
  6. In a traditional funeral, stand at the head of the casket as mourners pass by.<\/li>\n
  7. At the cemetery, walk ahead of the casket to the graveside.\u00a0 In some places, the pastor is expected to ride in the hearse.\u00a0 Otherwise, drive behind it to the cemetery.\u00a0 You are, in a sense, the \u201cspiritual guardian\u201d \u2013 no, I can\u2019t explain it, but people are equating you with Jesus, walking with their loved one to the grave.<\/li>\n
  8. Make the committal short, thank people for traveling this last mile \u2013 then, if you have a boutonniere, remove it and place it on the casket.\u00a0 Then, go to each primary family member and assure them of your prayers for them and their family.<\/li>\n
  9. If you are offered an honorarium, don\u2019t make a big deal out of it.\u00a0 Just say thank you and that it was an honor to serve.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
